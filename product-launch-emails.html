Production rules

Format locks

Send from
Ann's name (not brand)
Format
Plain text feel, no banner image, no CTA button (email 1, 2). Inline CTA link in 3 + 4.
Merge tag
{{ first_name }} at start of every email. Fallback to "there" if blank.
Image
Optional blurred/silhouette in 1+2. Crisp shot of both products in 3+4.
Platform
Klaviyo single sends
Audience
Existing customers + email subscribers (segment exclusive)
Cadence
Day 1 → Day 3 → Day 6 → Day 7-8 launch
Reply tracking
Email 1+2 invite replies. Count guesses, callout patterns in email 2.

Locks needed before send

  • Exact launch date (so Day-X math holds)
  • Travel bottle 100ml confirmed (locked in copy)
  • Refill pack 500ml confirmed (locked in copy, "covers two bottles")
  • Confirm $39 bundle price + free-travel-bottle language
  • Confirm "limited first run" framing is true
  • Set launch [DAY DATE] + [TIME] placeholders in email 3
  • Insert product link in email 4 body + CTA
  • Klaviyo segment built: existing customers + subscribers
  • Suppress this segment from EOFY sale blast (don't double-pitch)

Why this sequence works

  • → Travel = easy guess. Refill = cryptic ("not a replacement, just a little something on the side"). Tension drives reply rate.
  • → Reply invites in 1+2 = list warm-up before launch. Klaviyo engagement score climbs.
  • → "Bigger than the sale" anchor preserves EOFY scarcity. Different audience, different offer architecture.
  • → Limited first run = real scarcity (not manufactured).
  • → Founder voice + plain text = matches Mother's Day pattern that worked.
  • → 7-month gratitude anchor = founder credibility + community thank-you, opens door to "first dibs" offer naturally.

Burned / don't do

  • Subject lines starting "Big news" / "Drumroll" / "Guess what?" (too marketing-y)
  • Em dashes (Ann scrub rule, use commas/periods/"and")
  • Big hero banner image (kills plain-text feel)
  • CTA button on email 1+2 (kills the tease)
  • Reveal product in email 1 image (defeats guessing mechanic)
  • "Easy hint / harder hint" labels (use "Product one / Product two")
  • Iron joke in email 2 (cut, too punchy for the moment)
  • "That's the offer. That's it." (sounds dismissive of a good deal)
  • "Stop buying a new bottle every time you run out" (too negative, cut)
  • P.S. about "reason I'm telling you first" in email 1 (over-explains)
  • Hard sell language ("DON'T MISS OUT", "Buy now")
  • Sending to full list — exclusive offer is the whole hook
