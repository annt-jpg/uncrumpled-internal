Here's where we're going.

For the next era of content. From product demos → community hub for our people. Read this once. Reference whenever you film.

From Ann · For our content creator

TL;DR — What's changing

Until now, our content has been product-led. Hero shots, demos, features. It worked. We sold spray.

Now we're building a hub — a place people come because they recognise themselves, not because they need a wrinkle fix. That recognition is what makes them buy and stay.

The product still exists. It still gets shown. But it lives inside the chaos, not on a pedestal. We are now an identity brand. Spray is one thing we sell. Belonging is the bigger one.

Meet our person — Type C / Certified Chaos

She is everyone. She is the audience. She is also Ann.
CC

Certified Chaos

Internally we call her Type C. Publicly she's Certified Chaos — because nobody wants to claim a label that sounds clinical, but everyone wants to claim one that sounds fun.

"They call it Type C. We call it Certified Chaos."

She's the one whose brain runs hot. Whose drawer has a tooth in it ("mine? hopefully?"). Who plans to spring-clean the wardrobe and ends up doom-scrolling for 3 hours. Who has The Chair™ in her bedroom and a junk room that "used to be a guest room. 2019."

She's not lazy. She's not disorganised. She's wired differently — and the world that builds for Type B people calls it a flaw. We call it her actual personality.

What we want Uncrumpled to be

A hub, not a store.

Right now, if someone follows us, they get a feed of this product, watch the demo, here's what it can do. Useful — but they unfollow once they've bought.

What we want: a feed that someone follows because they recognise themselves in it. The Junk Drawer. The 11pm plans. The "5 minutes!" lie. The wardrobe full of nothing to wear.

If we get this right, the product mention becomes a relief — "wait, you actually made something for me?" — instead of a sales pitch they have to scroll past.

Think of Uncrumpled as the group chat for Certified Chaos people. Sometimes someone shares a deal on a great product. Mostly, we just laugh at our own mess together.

Voice rules — how we talk now

Apply to captions, on-screen text, voice-over, scripts.
Pronoun
Us, we, ours. Founder talks with the audience, not at them. "Made the spray so we'd never iron again" — not "for you to never iron again."
Product naming
Spray. Always. Never steam, steamer, iron-killer. We are a spray brand.
Identity callout
Type C = the wiring (used in clinical/internal language). Certified Chaos = the public claim, the badge. Use both — sometimes one, sometimes the other.
Tone
Self-aware, funny, never bitter. "I've spent 14 mins ironing one shirt and walked into coffee on it" — yes. "I'm such a hot mess" — no (negative, overused).
Length
2 lines max per slide / on-screen overlay. Captions ≤ 2 lines + 1 CTA.
Sentence shape
Grandiose claim → reality undercut. "I'll spring-clean the wardrobe — followed by deep regret 12 minutes in." That's the rhythm.
Banned words
"perfect / perfection / scientifically / iron-killer / hot mess / clean girl / Type C girls / Crumpled Club." Each has a reason — ask Ann.
Validation rule
We never lecture or correct our audience. We say "not all messy is lazy" and let them feel seen. Tiimo / Voomie energy, not productivity-guru.

Don't film these

Burned formats. Each has a reason.

Do not produce

  • Polished demo: "Watch the magic of spray un-wrinkle this shirt." Studio light, slow-mo. Old era.
  • Studio talking head about product features. Spray as the subject. We are now the subject.
  • Before/after wrinkle reveal as the climax. Product can appear, but never as the punchline.
  • "5 hacks for moms" listicle. Burned. Overused. Generic.
  • Cleaning videos / time-lapse tidy-ups. Cliché ADHD-tok. Not us.
  • Aesthetic clean-girl messy. Beige, neutral, performatively casual. Not chaos. Pretending.
  • "Type C girls — this is for us." Burned line. Don't revive.
  • "Crumpled Club" / "Hot Mess Express." Burned. Naff.
  • Manychat funnel / chatbot content. Loses the personability.
  • Lecturing tone. "Here's how to organise your wardrobe." We don't fix our audience. We hang out with them.
