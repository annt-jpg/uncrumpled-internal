The shift

We're pivoting from product-led selling to brand-led community. Same product. Same audience. Different frame.

From

Brand that sells a spray

  • Demo content: "day 1, day 2, day 3 of spraying my shirt"
  • Polished infomercial-style paid ads
  • Content disconnected from brand voice (Bossy V3)
  • Every post = conversion push
  • Ann speaks AT customers about features
→
To

Home for Type Cs (that also sells a spray)

  • Community content: "this is how Type Cs actually live"
  • Raw, relatable, ambient-sound content
  • Every post laddered to one of 3 pillars
  • 75% emotional connection, 25% product close
  • Ann + customers + creators all speak as ONE tribe

Content mix: 25 / 75

For every 4 posts: 3 are brand content, 1 is product. The emotional content builds connection. The product content gives the rational reason to buy.

75% BRAND · Type C / Anti-Perfection / Founder
25% PRODUCT

The 25% product content isn't separate — it still uses the brand voice. A demo caption should still sound like a Type C wrote it. Example: "3 minutes before school drop-off. Spray, smooth, out." Not: "Uncrumpled removes wrinkles in 3 minutes using premium technology."

Pillar 1: Type C Haven · PRIMARY

The biggest pillar and the identity layer the whole brand sits inside. Every other pillar is a flavour of Type C. Reina — read this whole section twice before writing any caption.

What is Type C? (the short version)

Type A colour-codes her calendar. Type B goes with the flow. Type C is us — the girl who wants to be Type A, is functioning like Type B, and has a chair covered in clothes she wore once. She's always been here. We're just giving her a name.

What is Type C? (the long version — for Reina)

Type C is not a new personality type. She's always been here — an identity that's existed forever, just without a name. She's the woman who:

  • buys the planner and forgets to open it
  • has a colour-coded Google Calendar AND misses the dentist
  • texts "on my way!" while still in a towel
  • owns labelled storage bins with mystery items inside
  • is high-performing at work and cannot find her other shoe
  • wants the Pinterest life, runs the real life

She doesn't fit the polished "girlboss" archetype. She doesn't fit the carefree "cool-girl" archetype. She's competent AND chaotic at the same time. That's Type C.

The emotion we're tapping: relief. Recognition. "Oh. Other people live like this too." Type C content should always feel like a slow exhale, never a lecture.

A day in the life of a Type C (so Reina can picture her)

6:40am. Second alarm. She meant to wake on the first. She scrolls Instagram for 8 minutes because she's "still waking up."

7:15am. Kids' uniforms missing. Her work blouse is in the dryer, slept-on. The partner asks where his shirt is. She didn't wash it.

7:55am. She looks put-together in the mirror. She isn't. But from three metres, nobody can tell.

11:30am. She crushes a client meeting. Gets compliments. Goes back to her desk and eats biscuits from the work kitchen for lunch.

6:10pm. Picks up kids. The Chair is exactly how she left it. 263 unread emails. She stares at the washing machine. Decides tomorrow is another day.

9:48pm. In bed. Remembers she didn't sign the school form. Sets a reminder. Will forget the reminder.

The Venn diagram: Type C = part of Type A AND part of Type B

This is the core of why Type C is not alienating. Type C isn't a separate tribe — it's the overlap between the other two.

  • From Type A she borrows: the ambition, the standards, the fact that she cares what she looks like, the planner, the list, the want to have it together.
  • From Type B she borrows: the execution (messy), the shrug, the "it'll be fine," the ability to let things go (eventually), the acceptance of chaos.
  • What makes it Type C: she carries BOTH at once. Type A wants, Type B reality. That tension IS the identity.

So when we talk to Type C, we're talking to a part of every Type A and every Type B too. Everyone has a Type C corner. We just named it.

How Type A relates (without feeling excluded)

A "pure" Type A woman doesn't exist all day, every day. She has Type C moments she doesn't post:

  • Sunday night when she's behind on meal prep
  • The one corner of the kitchen drawer that is absolute chaos
  • Her handbag (nobody wants to see inside)
  • The Thursday her period + a big presentation land on the same day

How we include her: Type A reads our content and thinks "god I wish I let myself be like that" OR "okay that's me on a bad day." Either way — in the door. She uses the spray because it lets her maintain A-level standards at B-level effort.

Tone rule for Type A mentions: always admiring + warm. "Type A could never" = playful, fine. "Type A is exhausting" = punching down, cut.

How Type B relates (without feeling called out)

Pure Type B exists too — but most self-described "Type B" women are actually Type C in disguise. They say "oh I'm so chill" then panic when they can't find the good mascara.

  • She has standards. She just doesn't always show them.
  • She wants to look put-together at the wedding / school drop / work thing, too.
  • She's not actually unbothered — she's decided effort isn't worth it for most things.

How we include her: Type B reads our content and finally feels seen as someone who does care about presentation, just on her own terms. She uses the spray because it matches her "minimum effort, maximum result" ethos. Tone rule: never "Type B is lazy." It's "Type B is smart about where she puts her energy."

Why Type C is our primary pillar

  • It's invitational — no demographic gate. Anyone can claim Type C on any day.
  • It contains A and B — nobody is excluded. It's the overlap, not a third silo.
  • It's an identity, not a product claim. Builds community that a spray alone never would.
  • It gives every other pillar a lens. Anti-Perfection = Type C reality. Founder Lens = Type C founder.
  • It solves the content problem — every post has a ready-made "is this a Type C thing?" filter.

How to talk TO Type C (not ABOUT Type C) — voice guide

Use "us" + "we": "this is for us" / "we've all been there" / "for those of us who…"

Use the listener's POV: "if your Chair currently has 6 outfits on it…" / "if you've ever sent 'on my way' still in a towel…"

Avoid "them": "Type C girls are…" is observational + distant. "We Type Cs…" is in-the-room + warm.

Avoid teaching tone: we're not explaining Type C to outsiders. We're recognising each other.

How Type A + Type B still feel included

Type C is invitational, not exclusionary. Everyone has a Type C corner. A and B are welcome to visit — most of them live here part-time anyway.

Rule 1 — never punch down at A or B. Every reference is warm + self-aware. "Type A could never" = playful. "Type A is uptight" = cut. Same for B.

Rule 2 — leave the door open. "You're welcome here even if you're mostly Type A." "Type B friends — you live here 80% of the time, come hang out."

Rule 3 — spotlight crossover moments. Feature a self-identified Type A having a Type C day. Feature a Type B realising she's actually Type C. Proves the door is open.

DO

  • Use "us" and "we" — make the audience the main character
  • Repost other Type C girls weekly
  • Define Type C visibly (pinned post, bio, caption)
  • Spotlight Type A and Type B having Type C moments
  • Stay self-deprecating, never other-deprecating
  • Ground every post in a specific scene, not an abstract trait

DON'T

  • Use the burned phrase "[type] girls — this is for us"
  • Gatekeep — never "real Type Cs do X"
  • Punch down at Type A or Type B
  • Over-explain the term in every caption (the pinned post does that job)
  • Make Type C content purely about Ann — the tribe matters more
  • Lecture. If it sounds like teaching, rewrite as recognising.

Pillar 2: Anti-Perfection & Mental Load

The realest pillar. Reality content that contrasts with the polished Instagram aesthetic. This is where Type C lives — so everything here should feel recognisable to a Type C within 2 seconds.

What this pillar covers

The day-to-day load of being a working mum / corporate woman. The mess nobody posts. The things that pile up. The specific moments that make Type Cs nod in recognition. Three sub-themes feed this pillar:

Sub-theme 1 — The Visible Mess

Physical chaos that lives in real life: The Chair, the basket, the counter, the car floor, the handbag. Visual, photogenic, easy to shoot.

Sub-theme 2 — The Invisible Load

Mental tabs nobody sees: remembering the vet, the school form, the partner's mum's birthday, the fact that the milk is out. Text-heavy, screenshot-heavy.

Sub-theme 3 — The Release Valve

Hacks, outsourcing, boundaries, "good enough" wins. Not perfection fixes — reduction of load. Product fits naturally here.

DO

  • Use real screenshots where possible — no mockups, no stock

  • Show the mess visually — phone camera is fine
  • Always land on a beat of relief or a small win

DON'T

  • Turn every post into a spray ad
  • Use stock imagery or overly styled Canva templates
  • Shame anyone for having it together sometimes
  • Make "mental load" feel like a lecture. It's recognition, not a TED talk.

Pillar 3: Founder Lens

Ann's voice. Low cadence. Raw, unpolished, phone-shot. The "resident Type C girl + founder" frame. 1-2 posts/month, not weekly. This pillar builds trust + face-of-brand, not volume.

Why it's capped at 1-2 posts/month

Ann is solo-running the business. Making Founder Lens daily content = new pressure she explicitly doesn't want. The whole strategy depends on this pillar staying sustainable.

Rule: if a Founder Lens shoot takes more than 20 minutes, kill it. Phone, one take, post. Polish = wrong vibe.

What Founder Lens does that the other pillars can't

  • Face of the brand. Type C Haven creates the identity. Founder Lens puts a human inside it.
  • Proves Ann IS Type C. Not a marketer pretending. Lived-in, not performed.
  • Parasocial pull. Followers feel they know her. Followers-who-feel-they-know-you buy 3x more.
  • Trust for the spray. Harder to fake a spray that works if the founder's face is on it.

Community mechanic: Type Cs share back

The content engine that makes this sustainable. We don't create ALL the content — we curate and amplify what Type Cs out there are already doing.

How it works

  1. InviteWeekly post/story prompt: "Show me your Type C moment" or "Drop a Chair of Shame pic in replies." Low-ask, high-volume.
  2. CollectReina monitors DMs, tagged posts, story replies daily. Builds a Notion/Sheet of candidate posts with name + handle + link + type-of-content.
  3. CurateAnn approves the weekly feature(s). Looks for authentic, on-voice, visually clear content. No heavily filtered or brand-dressed submissions.
  4. Repost + captionReina schedules repost with Ann-voice caption tying it to brand language. Always credit @handle. Story + feed cross-post.
  5. NotifyDM the creator: "reposted you — thank you for being this week's Type C." Builds loyalty + signals they're part of the tribe.

Why this matters

This mechanic solves three problems at once:

  • Ann's load. Turning audience into contributors = less original content to produce.
  • Authenticity. Real customers/creators are more believable than branded content.
  • Flywheel. Creators who get reposted share the repost. Their followers discover us. Repeat.

Banned words — cut on sight

Perfection language

Scientific / corporate language

superior innovative premium technology solution professional results

Also banned (from existing ad feedback)

The phrase "[type] girls — this is for us" (where [type] is any adjective). Overused in earlier ad creative. Use fresh variants: "resident Type C," "Type C reality," "for the Type Cs," or just show don't tell.

Product language rules

Our product = spray. NOT steam. NOT iron.

Correct: spray, bottle, spritz, mist, spray-and-smooth.
Never write: steam, steamer, iron, ironing (except about the thing we replace).

Never lead with: eco / clean ingredient / non-toxic. These are supporting bullets, not hero angles.

Rollout — Phase 1: carousels only

We're starting with the lowest-friction format. Get the pillars working in carousels first. Scale to video later.

Why carousels first

Carousels = Canva + phone photos. No camera performance. No video editing. No voiceover. Fully doable by Reina with Ann's direction. Proves the pillars convert attention before we invest in harder formats.

Do NOT launch: Founder Lens talking-head, TikTok-native video, Reels with voiceover. All deferred to Phase 2.
